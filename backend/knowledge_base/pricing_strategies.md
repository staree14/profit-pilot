# SME Pricing and Discounting Best Practices Guide

## 1. The Perils of Uncontrolled Discounting
Many Small and Medium Enterprises (SMEs) use discounting as a primary tool to close sales and hit short-term volume targets. However, unchecked discounting represents a severe profit leak.
- **Margin Erosion**: A 10% discount on a product with a 30% gross margin requires a **50% increase in sales volume** just to generate the same absolute profit dollars.
- **Brand Devaluation**: Constant discounting trains customers to wait for deals and lowers their perceived value of the product or service.
- **Sales Rep Alignment**: Often, sales teams are incentivized on raw volume or top-line revenue rather than gross profit margins. This misalignment encourages reps to offer maximum discounts to hit quotas.

## 2. Best-Practice Discounting Policies
To prevent profit leakage, SMEs should implement structured pricing controls:
- **Pre-Approved Tiers**: Restrict sales rep autonomy. For example, allow up to 5% discount without approval, 5-15% requires sales manager approval, and >15% requires CFO/Owner approval.
- **Volume-Based Caps**: Link discounts strictly to purchase volume. Volume discounts should follow a standard step-tier (e.g., buy 100+ units for 5% off, 500+ for 10% off).
- **Time-Bound Promotions**: Ensure discounts have hard expiry dates and are associated with specific promotional campaigns rather than persistent contract rates.
- **Bundle over Discount**: Instead of discounting a single product, bundle it with higher-margin accessories or service support. This preserves the core product price point.
- **Discount Auditing**: Regularly run reports on discount distribution per customer and sales agent to identify abuse or outlier accounts.
